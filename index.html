Welcome to PROS Worship

In PROS worship, we gather around the conviction that God speaks through His Word—and that His Word addresses the deepest questions of human life: Who am I? Why am I here? What is wrong with the world? Is there hope?

Rather than rushing to answers, this service invites us first to listen. Through the public reading of Scripture, we place ourselves under God's voice—trusting that the story of Jesus Christ gives meaning, direction, and hope that no other story can provide.

Here, the Bible is not merely discussed or summarized, but heard aloud, shaping us together as God's people. Whether you come with faith, doubt, curiosity, or longing, you are welcome here.

Why Scripture Reading Is the Center of Our Worship

At the heart of PROS worship is a simple conviction: God speaks through His Word. When Scripture is read aloud, God addresses His people—shaping faith, forming the church, and directing our lives.

The public reading of Scripture is not preparation for worship—it is worship. Before we speak, explain, or respond, we listen. Christians have always been a listening people.

A Biblical Pattern

From beginning to end, Scripture shows God's people gathering to hear God's Word read publicly:

  • Moses read the Law aloud before Israel Exodus 24:7; Deuteronomy 31:11–13
  • Ezra read the Scriptures for hours while the people listened attentively Nehemiah 8:1–3, 8
  • Jesus participated in public Scripture reading in the synagogue Luke 4:16–21
  • Paul commanded the church to prioritize public Scripture reading 1 Timothy 4:13; Colossians 4:16; 1 Thessalonians 5:27

The Practice of the Early Church

The early church clearly made reading the Scriptures central to their worship. In the second century, Justin Martyr described Christian worship like this:

"And on the day called Sunday, all who live in cities or in the country gather together to one place, and the memoirs of the apostles or the writings of the prophets are read, as long as time permits; then, when the reader has ceased, the president verbally instructs, and exhorts to the imitation of these good things."

— Justin Martyr, First Apology, ch. 67

A generation later, Tertullian described Christian gatherings in North Africa:

"We assemble to read our sacred writings… With the sacred words we nourish our faith, we animate our hope, we make our confidence more steadfast."

— Tertullian, Apology, ch. 39 (Roberts–Donaldson translation)

By the time of Augustine of Hippo in the fourth century, this pattern remained firmly in place. Reflecting on Christian worship, Augustine spoke of the church being fed by Scripture before anything else:

"The divine Scriptures are read, the psalms are sung, the sermons are preached, the prayers are offered."

— Augustine, Sermon 227

Across centuries and cultures, the church gathered first to listen.
